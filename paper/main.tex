\begin{abstract}
This paper studies whether Genetic Algorithm (GA) techniques, standard or
co-evolutionary, improve the robustness of chess opening repertoires.
The repertoire is memorization-constrained, retaining up to 25 moves per
color, and must cover all opponent replies played in at least 15\% of
games at any reached position (closure-constrained). We compare four
methodologies over 15 independent seeds: a greedy baseline that always
picks the most-played move, a greedy hill-climber
(\textsc{Greedy\_Hillclimb}) that begins greedy and accepts any fitness-
improving mutation, a standard GA (\textsc{Static}) evaluated against a
fixed uniform opponent mixture, and a co-evolutionary GA
(\textsc{Coevolve}) where the repertoire and opponent populations evolve
together. A temporal train/test split was applied; held-out evaluation
always uses a uniform opponent mixture regardless of training mode. Both
GA variants significantly outperform the greedy baseline approaches,
while \textsc{Coevolve} shows a consistent positive trend over
\textsc{Static} ($A_{12}=0.60$, $p=0.49$) that falls short of
statistical significance, traced to train-eval distributional symmetry.
\end{abstract}

chess opening repertoire, genetic algorithm, co-evolutionary optimization,

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Chess opening preparation can be fundamentally modelled as a constrained
memorization problem. An opening repertoire is a pre-planned set of moves
a player memorizes before a game. Each move played has successive resulting
moves that could be played by the opponent in response, and the branching
sequence formed represents a tree of decisions. A club player can at most
memorize some dozen moves and their branched outputs for a game to prepare
beforehand. Hence, for players rated between 1600 and 1800 by FIDE,
picking the right set of opening lines is a real and practical challenge
that can have a direct impact on how well they perform in games. The
problem centers on the exponentially increasing branching factor of the
moves and their responses, and the finite memorization budget a player
possesses. This makes opening preparation an instance of constrained
combinatorial optimization under uncertainty.

What makes this hard is the sheer number of possible choices. A pool of
opening lines built from real game data can easily contain hundreds of
positions. Picking 20 to 40 lines that together cover all important
opponent responses, without too much overlap, involves billions of possible
combinations. Simply searching through all of them is not feasible. Using
the lines with the highest win rates ignores gaps in coverage and the fact
that different opponents play very differently. Manually putting together a
repertoire depends heavily on personal preference and expert knowledge,
making it hard to reproduce or evaluate fairly. Moreover, what makes a
repertoire ``good'' is not fixed: lines that work well against a slow,
positional opponent might fail against an aggressive player, and a
repertoire tuned to perform well on average can still have weaknesses
against specific playing styles that overall statistics simply hide.

We treat the problem as \emph{robust constrained optimization}, where
the repertoire should perform well not just against fixed parameters, but
against adversarial opponent mixtures drawn from a realistic distribution.
The key mechanisms are: (i)~a memorization budget of 25 decision nodes per
color; (ii)~a closure rule forcing coverage of all opponent replies played
in ${\geq}15\%$ of games; (iii)~a CVaR-based fitness function penalizing
the expected score in the worst rating band; (iv)~three rating bands
(1000--1399, 1400--1799, 1800--2199); and (v)~truncated FEN strings so
that positions reached by different move orders but resulting in identical
board states are treated as the same node.

Repertoires are selected from a pool of 4610 unique opening lines from
Lichess game data, each candidate encoded as a directed acyclic graph.
Fitness is measured through a recursive walk function. Data after June 2025
is held out for testing; held-out evaluation always uses a uniform opponent
mixture regardless of training mode.

The central design question is what opponent model to train against. A
standard GA evaluates against static opponents but may overfit to fixed
caricatures. A co-evolutionary model lets the opponent population evolve
alongside the repertoire, probing worst-case scenarios continuously. We
ask which methodology yields optimal results: static evolution, adversarial
co-evolution, or the greedy baseline. We implemented the full pipeline with
explicit diversity-preserving mechanisms and ran 75 total experiments.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Self-Learning Evolutionary Chess Programs}

Fogel et al.~\cite{fogel2004} present a chess engine that evolves its
evaluation parameters---material values, a positional value table, and
neural networks---using GA through random mutation and competitive parent
selection. Their player Blondie25 attained a performance rating of around
2550 under tournament conditions. Our paper adopts their hyperparameter
heuristics, including the number of trials and generations.

\subsection{Co-Evolutionary Genetic Algorithms}

Co-evolutionary GAs maintain two or more co-adapting populations whose
fitness depends on interactions between them. Hillis~\cite{hillis1990}
showed that co-evolving sorting networks against test-case adversary
populations produced shorter networks than evolving against a fixed test
set. Rosin and Belew~\cite{rosin1997} formalized adversary
\emph{informativeness} and introduced Hall-of-Fame archives to prevent
loss of historically best opponents. Both ideas inform our implementation.

Ramos, Salgado, and Rosa~\cite{ramos2016} build a co-evolutionary chess
engine with separate populations for White and Black, scored using a
static board evaluation function. Their experiments provide practical
guidance: crossover probability 0.7 and bit mutation rate 0.02 work well
for binary chess chromosomes, and more generations with smaller populations
generally outperforms fewer generations with larger ones. We adopt their
hyperparameter choices as starting values.

A well-documented failure mode of co-evolutionary systems is
\emph{diversity collapse}, where tournament selection causes the opponent
population to converge toward a narrow region, making the adversarial
signal uninformative. Techniques for maintaining diversity include fitness
sharing, niching, novelty search~\cite{lehman2011}, and explicit crowding
mechanisms. We use novelty-weighted opponent fitness plus a hard reinit
trigger when mean pairwise distance falls below a threshold.

\subsection{Opponent Modeling}

Walczak~\cite{walczak1996} shows that building an opening book by modeling
opponent move tendencies requires position-aware predictions: accuracy on
Black's moves dropped from 100\% to 40\% when White's preceding move was
ignored. In our paper, move frequencies are tied to specific board
positions rather than estimated in isolation.

\subsection{Robust Optimization and CVaR}

Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR;~\cite{rockafellar2000}) penalizes the
worst-$\alpha$ tail of a score distribution rather than its expectation.
With $\alpha=1/3$ over three rating bands, CVaR collapses to the
worst-band score, a natural robustness objective. Our fitness is:
\begin{equation}
  f = \bar{s} + \lambda \cdot \mathrm{CVaR}, \quad \lambda = 1.0,
  \label{eq:fitness}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{s}$ is mean score and CVaR is the minimum score over bands.
$\lambda=0$ recovers mean-only; $\lambda\to\infty$ recovers minimax.

\subsection{Chess Opening Complexity from Online Data}

De Marzo and Servedio~\cite{marzo2023} use large-scale Lichess data to
study relationships between chess openings, finding ten distinct opening
clusters with a complexity metric that has a 0.64 Spearman correlation
with player Elo. This supports our use of Lichess data as the main source
and motivates the memorization-cost component of our fitness function.

\section{Problem Description}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{What Is a Chess Opening Repertoire?}

A chess opening \emph{repertoire} is a pre-planned decision tree for the
opening phase of the game. For each position where it is the player's
turn, the repertoire specifies a distinct response. On opponent turns, all
likely replies must be covered; otherwise the pre-committed decision ends
and the player must improvise.

\subsection{Why a Genetic Algorithm?}

The space of valid repertoires is extremely large, discontinuous, and
non-convex. Changing one committed move can cascade: it may violate the
budget, invalidate shared transpositions, or open qualitatively different
lines. Gradient methods are inapplicable over discrete move choices, and
exact search is infeasible given ${\approx}4{,}610$ nodes with branching
factors up to~10.

Genetic algorithms handle these factors naturally. Discrete mutation
operators change or delete individual moves or entire leaf trees, each
followed by closure repair. Pivot-crossover combines opening lines from
two parents. A population of 50 candidates explores the space in parallel,
preserving diversity when fitness gradients are weak. Importantly, a
co-evolving opponent population can be maintained at no extra architectural
cost, providing adaptive adversarial pressure.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:formulation}

\subsection{Data}

We explore the Lichess Opening Explorer~\cite{lichess} from the starting
position to ply depth~10, collecting per-band move statistics for rapid
and classical time controls. Crawl thresholds: minimum move frequency 5\%,
minimum game counts tightening with depth (5{,}000 for ply $\leq3$,
20{,}000 for ply $\leq6$, 50{,}000 for ply~$>6$). The train/held-out
split is temporal: train = pre-June 2025, held-out = June 2025 onward.
Both splits expose ${\approx}4{,}610$ positions.

\subsection{Chromosome Representation}

A repertoire for one color is a pair:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{committed}: a map from board positions (FEN strings) to
        chosen moves (UCI strings), with $|\texttt{committed}|\leq 25$.
  \item \texttt{reached}: positions reachable by following
        \texttt{committed} and any covered opponent reply.
\end{itemize}

The \texttt{reached} set is constructed by the \textbf{closure rule}:
starting from the initial position, whenever a committed move is followed
by an opponent reply played in ${\geq}15\%$ of games, that reply position
is added to \texttt{reached}, and the rule applies recursively. This
prevents the repertoire from committing a move while leaving the player
unable to respond to the most common replies.

\subsection{Repertoire Fitness}

The \emph{walk function} scores a repertoire under one band's policy
recursively: at our-turn nodes, follow the committed move; at
opponent-turn nodes, weight every covered child by $\pi_b$. The per-band
score is the average of White's walk score and $(1-\text{Black's walk
score})$. For an opponent mixture $\mathbf{p}\in\Delta^3$:
\begin{align}
  \bar{s}   &= \sum_b p_b\,s_b, \label{eq:mean}\\
  \mathrm{CVaR} &= \min_b s_b, \label{eq:cvar}\\
  f         &= \bar{s} + \lambda\cdot\mathrm{CVaR}. \label{eq:fit2}
\end{align}
Held-out evaluation always uses the uniform mixture
$\mathbf{p}=(1/3,1/3,1/3)$ regardless of how training was run.

\subsection{Genetic Operators}

\textbf{Mutation} applies one of four operators chosen uniformly, then
performs closure repair. If a mutation exceeds the budget or violates
closure, a \texttt{MutationFailed} flag is raised and mutation is retried
up to 5 times; if all fail, the individual is cloned unchanged.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{mutate\_move\_swap}: pick a committed node, swap its move
        to a different one; rebuild the subtree below.
  \item \texttt{mutate\_extend}: add a committed move at an uncovered
        our-turn position within \texttt{reached}.
  \item \texttt{mutate\_prune}: remove a committed move and its
        descendants to free budget.
  \item \texttt{mutate\_opening\_replacement}: replace the entire subtree
        below a shallow (ply $\leq4$) position with a different opening
        system.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Crossover} (pivot-crossover): find a position that both parents
have committed the same move to; splice parent B's subtree below that
pivot and graft it into parent A's repertoire, then apply closure repair
and budget enforcement. The subtree lives in a DAG because chess positions
can be reached via different move orders (transpositions); a position
shared by two paths is only removed if genuinely unreachable after the
graft.

\textbf{Selection}: tournament selection, size~4.

\subsection{Opponent Representation and Co-Evolution}

An opponent is a probability vector $\mathbf{p}\in\Delta^3$ with one
weight per rating band (Table~\ref{tab:bands}). The opponent regimes are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textsc{Static}: one uniform opponent $(1/3,1/3,1/3)$.
  \item \textsc{Coevolve}: 50 opponents co-evolved via tournament
        selection (size~4), Dirichlet-resample crossover (concentration
        scale~2.0), and Dirichlet-noise mutation (strength~0.8,
        rate~0.5). A Hall-of-Fame of size~10 retains the most
        \emph{informative} past opponents.
\end{itemize}

Throughout evolution the opponent population searches the simplex for the
hardest skill-mix combinations to challenge repertoires. Opponent fitness
is:
\begin{equation}
  f_{\mathrm{opp}} = \text{exploitation} + \omega\cdot\text{diversity},
  \quad \omega=1.0,
  \label{eq:oppfit}
\end{equation}
where exploitation is the negative repertoire score under that opponent
and diversity is the mean $\ell_2$ distance from all other opponents.
Two additional diversity mechanisms: (i)~pairs of new opponents closer
than $\ell_2=0.05$ trigger crowding replacement with a new random
opponent; (ii)~if mean pairwise $\ell_2$ falls below~0.3, the worst~40\%
of opponents are replaced.

\subsection{Why Co-Evolution Does Not Achieve Significance}

Despite stable opponent diversity, \textsc{Coevolve} does not
significantly outperform \textsc{Static}. The structural reason is
\emph{train-eval distributional symmetry}: training fitness and held-out
evaluation both reduce to expected score under the same uniform
rating-band mixture $(1/3, 1/3, 1/3)$. Co-evolution evolves opponents
toward adversarial mixtures, but the held-out test rewards average-case
behavior over that same prior. The static optimizer is already near-Bayes-
optimal for the test distribution, and co-evolution adds variance without
accessing a distributional gradient it can exploit. This is a fundamental
mismatch between the training signal and the evaluation criterion.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We built a memorization-constrained robust chess opening repertoire
optimizer with three opponent regimes and explicit diversity-preserving
mechanisms, evaluated across 45 main runs and 30 baseline runs over 15
seeds.

\textbf{What works.} The GA framework substantially outperforms the
most-played-move heuristic across all configurations. The population earns
its cost: a greedy hill-climber with the same budget loses to the full
population-based GA in ${\approx}70\%$ of paired comparisons. The closure
rule is confirmed load-bearing by ablation.

\textbf{What diversity preservation achieves.} Novelty-weighted opponent
fitness, crowding replacement, and diversity-threshold reinit successfully
prevent opponent-population collapse. Opponent diversity remains stable at
${\approx}0.52$ throughout 100 generations, ensuring adversarial pressure
is sustained.

\textbf{What diversity preservation does not achieve.} Despite stable
opponent diversity, \textsc{Coevolve} does not significantly outperform
\textsc{Static} ($p=0.49$, $A_{12}=0.60$). The bottleneck is structural:
train-eval distributional symmetry eliminates the information gradient
co-evolution needs. A real train/test distribution shift would give
co-evolution the asymmetry it needs to demonstrate a clear advantage.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}

\textbf{Asymmetric train/test distribution.} Train against one rating-band
mixture (e.g., overweighting the lowest band) and evaluate on a different
mixture to give co-evolution an exploitable gradient.

\textbf{Larger graph.} Crawling to ply~12 would expose more opening
systems and potentially widen the performance gap between methods.

\textbf{Stronger diversity mechanisms.} NSGA-II non-dominated sorting on
exploitation and novelty simultaneously, MAP-Elites over the simplex, or a
tabu archive for opponents could maintain diversity more aggressively.

\textbf{Engine ground truth.} Cross-referencing held-out win-rates with
Stockfish evaluations would separate ``exploits human tendencies'' from
``is objectively stronger.''

\textbf{Multi-color co-evolution.} A joint candidate co-evolving White and
Black simultaneously against a shared opponent population could discover
synergistic opening choices across colors.
