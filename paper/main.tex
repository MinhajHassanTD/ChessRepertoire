\begin{abstract}
We study whether diversity-preserving co-evolutionary adversarial
training improves the robustness of memorization-constrained chess
opening repertoires.  A repertoire commits to at most 25 moves per
color and must cover any opponent reply played in ${\geq}15\%$ of
games at a reached position (the \emph{closure rule}).  We compare
four methods across 15 independent seeds: a greedy most-played-move
heuristic, a static GA trained against a uniform rating-band mixture
(\textsc{Static}), a greedy hill-climber (\textsc{Greedy\_Hillclimb})
with equal evaluation budget, and a co-evolved opponent population
with explicit diversity preservation (\textsc{Coevolve}).

All GA variants significantly outperform the most-played-move heuristic
(held-out mean $0.526$ vs.\ $0.494$, $p<0.001$, $A_{12}=0.00$).
Unlike na\"\i ve co-evolution, the diversity-preserving implementation
keeps opponent behavioral diversity stable throughout 100 generations
(mean pairwise $\ell_2$ distance ${\approx}0.52$, flat from
generation~0 to~100).  Under this regime, \textsc{Coevolve} shows
a positive trend over \textsc{Static} (mean $0.5260$ vs.\ $0.5255$,
$A_{12}=0.60$) and the full population-based GA beats the hill-climber
(\textsc{Static} vs.\ \textsc{Greedy}: $A_{12}=0.70$).  However,
neither gap reaches statistical significance at $\alpha=0.05$
(\textsc{Coevolve} vs.\ \textsc{Static}: $p=0.49$).  We trace the
persistent null result to train-eval distributional symmetry: both
training fitness and held-out evaluation reduce to expected score under
the same uniform rating-band mixture, leaving co-evolution no
distributional shift to exploit.  A closure-rule ablation confirms the
novel structural constraint is load-bearing (removal costs
${\approx}0.008$ held-out score, $p<0.001$).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\IEEEPARstart{C}{hess} opening preparation is fundamentally a
constrained memorization problem.  A club-level player can realistically
commit a few dozen specific positions to long-term memory, yet the
branching tree of plausible opponent replies within the first five moves
alone runs into thousands of positions.  This tension---finite memory,
exponential branching---makes opening preparation an instance of
constrained combinatorial optimization under uncertainty.

The standard human approach is deterministic and fragile: memorize the
moves most often played at your rating level.  This heuristic ignores
two structural problems.  First, a move that is popular on average may
perform poorly against specific sub-populations of opponents (e.g.,
players in a particular rating band who favor unusual lines).  Second,
it treats every opponent reply as equally likely, even rare ones that can
be catastrophic if encountered unprepared.

We treat the problem as \emph{robust constrained optimization}: build a
repertoire that performs well not just in expectation but against
adversarial opponent mixtures drawn from a realistic distribution.  The
key mechanisms are: (i)~a memorization budget of at most 25 committed
moves per color; (ii)~a \emph{closure rule} that automatically covers
any opponent reply played in ${\geq}15\%$ of games; and (iii)~a
CVaR-based fitness function that penalizes worst-case performance across
rating bands.

The central design question is \emph{what opponent model to train
against}.  A static uniform mixture is simple and unbiased but may miss
adversarial sub-distributions.  Co-evolutionary adversarial training---
evolving a population of opponents alongside repertoire candidates---
promises to discover the candidate's worst case dynamically~\cite{hillis1990,
rosin1997}.  We ask:

\begin{quote}
\emph{Under what conditions does diversity-preserving co-evolutionary
adversarial training improve generalization for memorization-constrained
chess repertoire optimization?}
\end{quote}

We implement the full pipeline including explicit diversity-preservation
mechanisms, run 75 total experiments, and report results honestly.  The
answer is: co-evolution shows a consistent positive trend but fails to
achieve statistical significance, and we identify train-eval distributional
symmetry as the structural cause.

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A reproducible implementation of memorization-constrained robust
        repertoire optimization with diversity-preserving co-evolution:
        opponent crowding detection, diversity-threshold reinit, Hall-of-Fame
        (size~10), and novelty-weighted opponent fitness.
  \item A 75-run evaluation (15 seeds $\times$ 5 methods) on a held-out
        Lichess data slice, with non-parametric significance testing
        (Wilcoxon signed-rank, Holm-corrected, Vargha--Delaney $A_{12}$).
  \item An empirical demonstration that diversity preservation stabilizes
        opponent populations ($\ell_2{\approx}0.52$ flat vs.\ collapse to
        ${\approx}0.28$ in na\"\i ve co-evolution).
  \item A diagnosis of why the null result persists: train-eval distributional
        symmetry eliminates the information gradient co-evolution needs.
  \item A closure-rule ablation confirming the structural constraint is
        load-bearing.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work and Technical Background}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Co-Evolutionary Genetic Algorithms}

Co-evolutionary GAs maintain two or more co-adapting populations whose
fitness depends on interactions between them.  Hillis~\cite{hillis1990}
showed that co-evolving sorting networks against test-case adversary
populations produced shorter networks than evolving against a fixed test
set.  Rosin and Belew~\cite{rosin1997} formalized adversary
\emph{informativeness}---the degree to which an opponent reveals
performance variation across candidates---and introduced Hall-of-Fame
archives to prevent loss of historical pressure.  Both ideas inform our
implementation.

A well-documented failure mode of co-evolutionary systems is
\emph{diversity collapse}: under tournament selection the opponent
population converges toward a narrow region of the simplex, so the
adversarial signal becomes uninformative and selection degenerates toward
optimization against a single representative opponent.  Techniques for
maintaining diversity include fitness sharing, niching, novelty
search~\cite{lehman2011}, and explicit crowding mechanisms.  We use
novelty-weighted opponent fitness plus a hard reinit trigger when mean
pairwise distance falls below a threshold.

\subsection{Robust Optimization and CVaR}

Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR;~\cite{rockafellar2000}) penalizes the
worst-$\alpha$ tail of a score distribution rather than its expectation.
With $\alpha=1/3$ over three rating bands, CVaR collapses to the
worst-band score---a natural robustness objective when the agent must
perform across multiple opponent sub-populations.  Our fitness is:
\begin{equation}
  f = \bar{s} + \lambda \cdot \mathrm{CVaR}, \quad \lambda = 1.0,
  \label{eq:fitness}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{s}$ is the mean score and CVaR is the minimum score over
bands.  $\lambda=0$ recovers mean-only; $\lambda\to\infty$ recovers
minimax.

\subsection{Constrained Combinatorial Optimization in Games}

Most published work on chess preparation assumes engine-grade evaluation
and unlimited memory.  The memorization budget makes the problem
tractable but non-trivial: a 25-move budget covers roughly 2--4\% of the
${\approx}1{,}300$-position opening graph, yielding a combinatorially
large discrete search space.  We evaluate using empirical win-rate data
from Lichess rather than engine evaluation, measuring what wins more
games against humans in each rating band.

\section{Problem Description}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{What Is a Chess Opening Repertoire?}

A chess opening \emph{repertoire} is a pre-committed decision tree for
the opening phase.  For each position where it is the player's turn, the
repertoire specifies exactly one response.  For opponent-turn positions,
all replies likely to be encountered must be covered---otherwise the
player exits preparation and must improvise.

Formally, a repertoire for one color is a pair:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{committed}: a map from board positions (FEN strings) to
        chosen moves (UCI strings), with $|\texttt{committed}|\leq 25$.
  \item \texttt{reached}: the set of positions reachable by following
        \texttt{committed} and any covered opponent reply.
\end{itemize}

The \texttt{reached} set is constructed by the \textbf{closure rule}:
starting from the initial position, whenever a committed move is followed
by an opponent reply played in ${\geq}15\%$ of games, that reply position
is added to \texttt{reached}, and the rule applies recursively.  This
prevents the repertoire from committing a move while leaving the player
unable to respond to the most common replies.

\subsection{Why a Genetic Algorithm?}

The space of valid repertoires is large, discontinuous, and non-convex.
Changing one committed move can cascade: it may violate the budget
(closure repair adds positions), invalidate shared transpositions, or
open qualitatively different lines.  Gradient methods are inapplicable
over discrete move choices, and exact search is infeasible given
${\approx}1{,}300$ nodes with branching factors up to~10.

Genetic algorithms handle this naturally.  Discrete mutation operators
change individual moves or entire opening systems, each followed by
closure repair.  Pivot-crossover splices a subtree at a shared position
between two parents, combining opening systems.  A population of 50
candidates explores the space in parallel, preserving diversity when
fitness gradients are weak.  Importantly, a co-evolving opponent
population can be maintained at no extra architectural cost.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:formulation}

\subsection{Data}

We crawl the Lichess Opening Explorer~\cite{lichess} from the starting
position to ply depth~8, collecting per-band move statistics for rapid
and classical time controls.  Crawl thresholds: minimum move frequency
5\%, minimum game counts that tighten with depth (5\,000 for ply
$\leq3$, 20\,000 for ply $\leq6$, 50\,000 for ply~$>6$).  The
train/held-out split is temporal: train~= pre-June~2025, held-out~=
June~2025 onward.  Both splits expose ${\approx}1{,}300$ positions.

\subsection{Per-Band Move Policies}

For each rating band $b$ and position $f$ we compute a smoothed policy:
\begin{equation}
  \pi_b(m\mid f)
    = \frac{\mathrm{count}_b(m,f)+\alpha\,\pi_{\mathrm{agg}}(m\mid f)}
           {\sum_{m'}\mathrm{count}_b(m',f)+\alpha},
  \quad \alpha=5.
  \label{eq:policy}
\end{equation}
This Laplace-style smoothing shrinks sparse per-band evidence toward the
aggregate distribution, falling back fully when a band has zero games at
a position.

\subsection{Empirical-Bayes Leaf Scores}

Each (position, band) pair receives a shrunk expected White score:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{s}_b(f) = \frac{n\cdot s_{\mathrm{raw}} + \tau\cdot\mu_0}{n+\tau},
  \quad \tau=20,
  \label{eq:shrinkage}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_0{\approx}0.52$ is the games-weighted aggregate score over
positions at ply $\leq8$.

\subsection{Repertoire Fitness}

The \emph{walk function} scores a repertoire under one band's policy
recursively: at our-turn nodes, follow the committed move (or return the
cached leaf if uncommitted); at opponent-turn nodes, weight every covered
child by $\pi_b$.  The per-band score is the average of White's walk
score and $(1-\text{Black's walk score})$.  For an opponent mixture
$\mathbf{p}\in\Delta^3$:
\begin{align}
  \bar{s}   &= \sum_b p_b\,s_b, \label{eq:mean}\\
  \text{CVaR} &= \min_b s_b, \label{eq:cvar}\\
  f         &= \bar{s} + \lambda\cdot\text{CVaR}. \label{eq:fit2}
\end{align}
Held-out evaluation always uses the uniform mixture
$\mathbf{p}=(1/3,1/3,1/3)$ regardless of how training was run.

\subsection{Genetic Operators}

\textbf{Mutation} (one of four operators chosen uniformly, then closure
repair):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{mutate\_move\_swap}: replace one committed move with a
        different legal move at the same position.
  \item \texttt{mutate\_extend}: add a committed move at an uncovered
        our-turn position within \texttt{reached}.
  \item \texttt{mutate\_prune}: remove a committed move and its
        descendants to free budget.
  \item \texttt{mutate\_opening\_replacement}: replace the entire subtree
        below a shallow (ply $\leq4$) position with a different opening system.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Crossover} (pivot-crossover): find a position both parents have
committed identically; splice parent B's subtree below that pivot into
parent A's repertoire, then apply closure repair and budget enforcement.

\textbf{Selection}: tournament selection, size~4.

\subsection{Opponent Representation and Co-Evolution}

An opponent is a weight vector $\mathbf{p}\in\Delta^3$ over rating bands.
The opponent regimes are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textsc{Static}: one frozen opponent $(1/3,1/3,1/3)$.
  \item \textsc{Greedy\_Hillclimb}: a $(1{+}1)$-ES starting from the greedy
        most-played-move solution.  Same budget as the GA ($50\times100=5\,000$
        fitness calls).
  \item \textsc{Coevolve}: 50 opponents co-evolved via tournament selection
        (size~4), Dirichlet-resample crossover (concentration scale~2.0),
        and Dirichlet-noise mutation (strength~0.8, rate~0.5).  A
        Hall-of-Fame of size~10 retains the most \emph{informative} past
        opponents.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Diversity preservation in \textsc{Coevolve}.}  Opponent fitness is:
\begin{equation}
  f_{\mathrm{opp}} = \text{exploitation} + \omega\cdot\text{diversity},
  \quad \omega=1.0,
  \label{eq:oppfit}
\end{equation}
where exploitation is the negative repertoire score under that opponent
and diversity is the mean $\ell_2$ distance from all other opponents.
Two additional mechanisms enforce diversity: (i)~pairs of new opponents
closer than $\ell_2=0.05$ trigger crowding replacement of the later
member with a fresh random opponent; (ii)~if mean pairwise $\ell_2$
falls below~0.3, the worst~40\% of opponents are replaced (diversity
reinit).

\subsection{Why Co-Evolution Does Not Achieve Significance}

Despite stable opponent diversity, \textsc{Coevolve} does not
significantly outperform \textsc{Static}.  The structural reason is
\emph{train-eval distributional symmetry}: the training fitness and the
held-out evaluation both reduce to expected score under the same uniform
rating-band mixture $(1/3, 1/3, 1/3)$.  Co-evolution evolves opponents
toward adversarial mixtures, but the held-out test rewards
average-case behavior over that same prior.  The static optimizer is
already near-Bayes-optimal for the test distribution, and co-evolution
adds variance without accessing a distributional gradient it can exploit.

This is not a failure of the diversity mechanisms---those work as
designed.  It is a fundamental mismatch between the training signal and
the evaluation criterion.  Any future co-evolutionary robustness claim
on this dataset must construct a genuine train/test distribution shift.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We built a memorization-constrained robust chess opening repertoire
optimizer with three opponent regimes and explicit diversity-preserving
mechanisms, evaluated across 45 main runs and 30 baseline runs over 15
seeds.

\textbf{What works.}  The GA framework substantially outperforms the
most-played-move heuristic across all configurations.  The population
earns its cost: a greedy hill-climber with the same budget loses to the
full population-based GA in ${\approx}70\%$ of paired comparisons.  The
closure rule is confirmed load-bearing by ablation.

\textbf{What diversity preservation achieves.}  Novelty-weighted opponent
fitness, crowding replacement, and diversity-threshold reinit successfully
prevent opponent-population collapse.  Opponent diversity remains stable
at ${\approx}0.52$ throughout 100 generations, ensuring adversarial
pressure is sustained.

\textbf{What diversity preservation does not achieve.}  Despite stable
opponent diversity, \textsc{Coevolve} does not significantly outperform
\textsc{Static} ($p=0.49$, $A_{12}=0.60$).  The positive trend is
consistent across both the uniform-mixture and worst-band metrics, but
falls short of statistical significance at $n=15$.  The remaining
bottleneck is structural: train-eval distributional symmetry eliminates
the information gradient co-evolution needs.

The overall picture: diversity preservation partially rescues
co-evolutionary dynamics, producing a consistent positive trend over the
static baseline.  A real train/test distribution shift would give
co-evolution the asymmetry it needs to demonstrate a clear advantage.

% ============================================================
\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}

\textbf{Asymmetric train/test distribution.}  Train against one
rating-band mixture (e.g., overweighting the lowest band) and evaluate on
a different mixture.  This gives co-evolution an exploitable gradient and
tests whether the positive trend becomes a significant win.

\textbf{Larger graph.}  Crawling to ply~12 would expose more opening
systems and potentially widen the performance gap between methods.

\textbf{Stronger diversity mechanisms.}  NSGA-II non-dominated sorting
on exploitation and novelty simultaneously, MAP-Elites over the simplex,
or a tabu archive for opponents could maintain diversity more aggressively.

\textbf{Engine ground truth.}  Cross-referencing held-out win-rates with
Stockfish evaluations would separate ``exploits human tendencies'' from
``is objectively stronger.''

\textbf{Multi-color co-evolution.}  A joint candidate that co-evolves
White and Black simultaneously against a shared opponent population could
discover synergistic opening choices across colors.
